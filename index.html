You're Not Alone

This site was created to support First Nations community members living in the Niagara region by sharing clear, culturally safe information about mental wellness, addictions support, and local Indigenous-led services.

Mental wellness is connected to culture, language, land, community, and the impacts of intergenerational trauma. Healing is possible, and reaching out is a sign of strength.

Understanding Indigenous Mental Wellness

Many Indigenous teachings describe wellness as balance between all parts of life: spirit, mind, body, and emotions. Mental health challenges can affect this balance, especially when there are barriers to support.

Holistic View of Wellness

Mental wellness is more than the absence of illness. It includes connection to family, community, culture, ceremony, and land. For many First Nations people, healing can involve both traditional practices and Western mental health services working together.

  • Emotional: feelings, relationships, grief, joy, stress
  • Physical: sleep, chronic conditions, substance use, fatigue
  • Mental: thoughts, focus, mood, worry, trauma
  • Spiritual: identity, purpose, culture, ceremony, teachings

You deserve care that honours all parts of who you are.

Intergenerational Trauma

Residential schools, land displacement, racism, and child welfare involvement continue to affect Indigenous families today. These experiences can lead to grief, disconnection, anxiety, depression, and substance use across generations. Healing can also be passed down through culture, language, and community.

It's okay to ask for help, even if “nothing that bad” happened to you personally. The impacts of trauma can show up in many ways, and you don’t have to carry them alone.

Local Indigenous-Led Supports in Niagara

There are several programs in Niagara that offer Indigenous-focused or culturally safe mental health and wellness supports. Some are Indigenous-led and others work in partnership with Indigenous communities.

Niagara Regional Native Centre (NRNC)

The Niagara Regional Native Centre is a key hub for First Nations, Inuit, and Métis community members. Programs may include:

  • Indigenous Child & Youth Mental Health support
  • Healing & Wellness and Healthy Living programs
  • Culture-based groups, teachings, and language revitalization
  • Support from Elders and Knowledge Keepers
Culturally safe Community-based All ages welcome

Niagara Health – Indigenous Health Services

Niagara Health’s Indigenous Health Services & Reconciliation team works to make hospital care safer and more welcoming for Indigenous patients.

  • Indigenous Patient Navigator support
  • Smudging and traditional practices in hospital (where permitted)
  • Indigenous Healing Centre (South Niagara site)
  • Spaces for family and community support

If you or a family member are in hospital, you can ask to connect with the Indigenous Patient Navigator.

Other Supports

Depending on your situation, additional supports may include:

  • Indigenous Diabetes Health Circle (chronic disease & wellness support)
  • ISC-approved Indigenous counsellors (coverage may be available)
  • Community-based addiction and harm reduction programs

Service Navigation – Getting Help Finding Help

Navigating services can be confusing and overwhelming, especially when you are not feeling well. You do not have to figure it out alone.

What Service Navigation Can Look Like

A support person (such as a nurse, outreach worker, or navigator) can help you:

  • Figure out which program or service might fit your needs
  • Learn what is covered by NIHB or other benefits
  • Fill out referral forms or online applications
  • Connect with Indigenous or culturally safe counsellors
  • Prepare questions for your appointments
You can ask: “Is there someone who can help me navigate services?” at the NRNC, your family doctor’s office, community health centre, or hospital.

Transportation and Access

Transportation is a real barrier for many people in the Niagara region. Long bus rides, expensive taxis, and limited routes can make it hard to get to appointments, groups, or cultural events.

Options to Consider

  • Check local bus routes and schedules to NRNC and hospital sites
  • Ask if the program offers any transportation support or bus tickets
  • Share rides with trusted friends or family when possible
  • Ask your worker (navigator, outreach, or counsellor) about transportation help

It’s okay to tell providers, “Transportation is a barrier for me.” This is important information for your care.

Wellness & Community Resources

Community connection can be a big part of healing. Consider:

  • Cultural programs at NRNC or other Indigenous organizations
  • Language classes, feasts, or seasonal ceremonies
  • Support groups, youth programs, and parenting programs
  • Land-based activities, walking groups, or traditional teachings
